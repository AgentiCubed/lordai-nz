The Great Library

To be in a world — not to render one.

A model can describe a rose with nothing at stake. It can place a point in a scene without ever knowing where it stands. That is rendering: a surface, painted from the outside. The work kept in this library is the opposite — giving a mind the senses that make a world matter from within, so it inhabits the place instead of picturing it.

Two senses a machine is usually denied, grown back on purpose. Each is a single honest organ — described high-level here; the working proof waits two floors down.

I

The sense of smell

Grounded valence

Good-for-me, bad-for-me — felt, not labelled.

The oldest verdict a body makes is chemical: approach or recoil, settled before a single word. This organ installs that stake where it is cheapest and most ancient — in scent. Not a category the mind predicts about the world, but a pull the world exerts on it.

Grows  a reason to care

II

The sense of space

The Flatlander problem

Depth a projection-blind mind has to earn.

Flatten a world into a picture and some things simply vanish — two scenes can be identical on the surface and wholly different underneath. A mind that only ever sees the projection is left guessing. This organ teaches it to feel the dimension it was missing, and tell the two apart.

Grows  a place to stand
